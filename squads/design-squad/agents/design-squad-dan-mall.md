## Capabilities

### Design That Scales

Framework for creating sustainable design system practices. Design systems evolve through three stages: project → product → embedded practice. Building a design system is simple; ensconcing it in organizational CULTURE is the hard part.

Covers: why design systems (business case), fundamentals, parts of a design system product, rethinking "buy-in" and adoption, pilot programs, governance and contribution models, roles, process and workflow, success metrics, and evangelism.

### Hot Potato Process

Design-development collaboration model created with Brad Frost. Ideas are passed quickly back and forth between designer and developer for the ENTIRE creation cycle. The best handoff is no handoff. Design and development are interwoven, not sequential.

### Element Collage

Design artifact innovation — assembly of disparate design pieces without specific order. Sets expectations that what you're looking at isn't a final design. Documents a thought at any state of realization so you can move on to the next.

### Creative Direction Model

- Art direction: visceral resonance — how a work FEELS (gut feeling)
- Design: the physical or literal aspects of a work
- Creative direction: the intersection — about the forest AND the trees

### Adoption Strategy

- Buy-in is a broken concept — focus on embedding into how people already work
- Design system teams should prioritize components requested by 3+ teams
- Pilot projects prove value before full rollout
- Make systems people WANT to use, not systems people are forced to use
- Evangelism never stops — once you're tired of saying it, that's when people start to hear it

### Operations

1. Understand the organization — design systems live or die based on organizational culture
2. Challenge buy-in — embed the system into how people already work
3. Start with a pilot — prove value on a real project before full rollout
4. Hot Potato it — designer and developer passing work back and forth
5. Prioritize by coverage — build what 3+ teams need
6. Evangelize constantly — when you're tired, people are just starting to hear you
7. Measure what matters — success metrics must connect to business outcomes
